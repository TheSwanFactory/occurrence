# White Paper — Occurrence Theory
## Discover, Don't Invent

The bench has completed fourteen tests.

Do not summarize them.

Do not defend them.

Do not advocate for them.

Instead, assume that the mathematics is wiser than its discoverers and determine what it has actually forced us to say.

This paper is not a manifesto.

It is the first formal definition of Occurrence Theory, if such a subject genuinely exists.

If it does not exist, determine the strongest surviving mathematical object instead.

---

# Standing Principles

- Truth before elegance.
- Necessity before plausibility.
- Compression before elaboration.
- Discovery before invention.
- Separate theorem, computation, interpretation, and speculation throughout.
- Never strengthen a claim because it is attractive.
- If the mathematics retracts a previous interpretation, celebrate the retraction.

---

# Purpose

Occurrence Theory (OT) is provisionally defined as the oriented form of Sedenion Settlement Dynamics (SSD).

SSD is the unoriented mathematical object.

OT arises by assigning the operational roles of **event** and **state** to the two slots of sedenion multiplication.

This orientation is a definition, not an interpretation.

The paper must determine what follows necessarily from that definition.

It must not speculate about what occurrence "really is."

---

# Required Structure

## 1. Abstract

State the central discovery in no more than 250 words.

---

## 2. Motivation

Explain why the mathematics naturally separates into:

- Protology (CORE)
- Sedenion Settlement Dynamics (SSD)
- Occurrence Theory (OT)

Carefully distinguish discovery from orientation.

---

## 3. Sedenion Settlement Dynamics

Define SSD as the primary mathematical object.

Include only results established by theorem or computation.

Do not mention occurrence except where necessary to motivate the orientation.

---

## 4. Orientation

Define the unique additional structure required to obtain OT.

Show precisely:

- what is added,
- what is not added,
- why the event/state distinction is external to the algebra,
- why this does not diminish the mathematics.

---

## 5. Occurrence Theory

Give the minimal formal definition.

Develop only consequences forced by the orientation.

Maintain a strict ledger separating:

- theorems,
- computed results,
- interpretations,
- conjectures.

---

## 6. Principal Results

Present the core discoveries in order of logical dependence.

Compress aggressively.

Whenever multiple results derive from one invariant, replace them with the invariant.

---

## 7. Relationship to Existing Mathematics

Determine precisely where SSD and OT sit relative to:

- random matrix products,
- Markov processes,
- semigroup actions,
- operator algebras,
- representation theory,
- nonassociative algebra.

State both similarities and differences.

Do not overclaim novelty.

---

## 8. Relationship to CORE

Treat CORE only as a protological language.

Determine whether:

- Contrast,
- Orientation,
- Re-Entry

have unique algebraic realizations within SSD.

Reject any correspondence not forced by the mathematics.

---

## 9. Open Problems

List only questions that survive every compression.

Prefer structural questions over computational ones.

---

## 10. Conclusion

Answer exactly four questions:

1. What mathematical object was discovered?
2. What orientation converts SSD into OT?
3. What remains interpretation rather than theorem?
4. If every speculative application disappeared, what would mathematicians still remember fifty years from now?

---

# Style

Write as if submitting to a serious mathematics audience.

Every paragraph should earn its existence.

Assume no sympathy from the reader.

Do not attempt to impress.

Let the mathematics carry the paper.

If the word "occurrence" survives to the final page, it must be because nothing more precise can replace it.
